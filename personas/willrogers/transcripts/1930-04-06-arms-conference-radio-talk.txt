Arms Conference Radio Talk
Date: 1930-04-06
Source: "Wit And Philosophy From The Radio Talks Of America's Humorist Will Rogers" (1930, public domain), via archive.org

OVERVIEW
--------
Will Rogers's radio talk on the 1930 disarmament conference, delivered in his familiar folksy, self-deprecating style — turning a serious topic into a story about geography and neighbors.

ATMOSPHERE
----------
Casual, warm, humorous radio broadcast. Rogers talking directly to his audience like he's chatting with a friend.

TRANSCRIPT
----------
Will Rogers: Just when I was bragging on the radio and thought there was such a wonderful entertainment here they had to lower themselves by resorting to giving a little outline of the story of my life. That shows you how hard up they are for entertainment.

Will Rogers: I started with Mr. Ziegfeld in 1914 as a child prodigy. I was just a little feller, and went in the Follies first on account of chewing gum. I chewed gum for Ziegfeld for twelve years, and never dropped a cud or anything. I was better than a cow that way.

Will Rogers: Now tonight all I know is this — just what little I read in the papers during the day. Every editorial you pick up is discontented with the way the disarmament conference is going, and they think Europe is very warlike and ought to go on and disarm. Now let's just kind of look it over and put ourselves in their position.

Will Rogers: Suppose you took Germany, for instance, and traded places with Mexico, and you took France and put them up with our good friends the Canadians, and England you moved to about where Cuba is, and Japan comes in around the Hawaiian Islands. Now just surround us with those four nations and see how much disarmament we start hollering about.

Will Rogers: You know I don't mind telling you brothers that geography has been mighty good to us. It's wonderful to pay honor to Washington and Lincoln, but I want to tell you we ought to lay out one day a year for the old boy that laid out the location of this country. I don't know who he was, but boy he was a sage, that bird was.

Will Rogers: Europe used to ask me, "Rogers, why is it you all are in so bad? Nobody seems to like America." So I had to admit we was in kinda bad. We are sort of the pole-cat of nations, you know. But then I'd ask them, offhand, just casually name me a list of your bosom friends among other nations. They can't hate us as bad as they hate each other.

Will Rogers: They think we are doing better than we are. They could be doing just as good as we are if they bought as much on credit as we do. They don't know you can have nice radios and automobiles and never pay for it. They are awfully funny that way.
